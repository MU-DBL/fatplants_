Summary from MetaCyc:

General Background

phytate (phytate, phytic acid) is one of the most prevalent forms of phosphorylated inositols in the eukaryotic cell, with a total concentration of 15-100 μM [Szwergold87, French91, Bunce93]. It's cellular distribution is not completely known - some of it is soluble, some is "wall-papered" around membranes, while some is bound to proteins [Shears98]. In plant seeds, large amount of phytate are complexed with cations and deposited as globular inclusions in membrane-bound storage bodies [Coelho05].

The compound has been proposed to have a number of signaling roles, including regulation of insulin exocytosis [Efanov97], regulation of nuclear mRNA export [York99, Saiardi00], binding of the clathrin assembly proteins AP2 and AP3 [Theibert91, Chadwick92], inhibition of clathrin cage assembly [Norris95, Ye95], and inhibition of serine and threonine protein phosphatases that are thought to regulate L-type Ca2+ channels [Larsson97].

Phytate in Plants

In plant cells phytate serves as a storage form of myo-inositol, phosphate and mineral nutrients for utilization during seed germination and seedling growth. It is deposited within single-membrane storage organelles referred to as protein bodies and is usually present as mixed complexes with different cations, including K+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Fe2+, and Zn2+, known as phytin [Raboy01, Raboy96]. Although phytin only makes up about 1% of the seed weight, it may contain up to 80% of the total seed phosphorus [Coelho05]. Phytate is also present in other plant tissues and organs such as pollen, roots, tuber and turions in smaller quantities [Raboy03].

About This Pathway

The main function of phytate in yeast is related to regulation of mRNA export from the nucleus [York99, Odom00, Saiardi00].

The biosynthetic pathway found in yeast and plants proceeds from D-myo-inositol (1,4,5)-trisphosphate (produced by phospholipase C as described in D-myo-inositol (1,4,5)-trisphosphate biosynthesis) by a series of successive phosphorylations carried out by only two enzymes.

In yeast, IPK2 encodes inositol polyphosphate multikinase, a versatile enzyme that catalyzes the phosphorylation of D-myo-inositol (1,4,5)-trisphosphate at both the 3- and 6-positions, resulting in formation of both D-myo-inositol (1,3,4,5)-tetrakisphosphate and D-myo-inositol (1,4,5,6)-tetrakisphosphate. Similarly, the enzyme phosphorylates both of these compounds to D-myo-inositol 1,3,4,5,6-pentakisphosphate [Saiardi00]. In Arabidopsis thaliana very similar enzymes exist. The plant has two isoforms of IPK2, encoded by IPK2α and IPK2β [StevensonPaulik02].

D-myo-inositol 1,3,4,5,6-pentakisphosphate is substrate for the IPK1-encoded inositol (1,3,4,5,6)-pentakisphosphate 2-kinase, which adds a phosphate group at position 2, resulting in phytate. This enzyme is found in all eukaryotes, from yeast to mammals.

Yeast cells lacking IPK1 are deficient in phytate production and exhibit lethality when combined with a gle1 (encoding the nuclear pore protein GLE1, which is involved in RNA export from the nucleus) mutant allele [Ives00]. Plant IPK2β gene can rescue this mutant [StevensonPaulik02].

Humans and monogastric animals lack hydrolytic phytases in their digestive tract, and are unable to extract the minerals from it. Undigested phytate in animal waste is a major contributor to eutrophication and environmental phosphorus pollution [Raboy01, BrinchPedersen02].
