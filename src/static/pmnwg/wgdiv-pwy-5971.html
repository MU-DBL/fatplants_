General Background

Fatty acids are key building blocks for the phospholipid components of cell membranes. They are also determinants of intracellular communication in the form of lipid second messengers [Prieschl00] and protein modification, where fatty acyl moieties of proteins modify their location and function [Resh99].

Palmitate is one of the most common saturated fatty acids found in animals and plants. It was discovered by Edmond Fremy in 1840 in saponified palm oil, of which it is a major component, and hence its name. Palmitate is synthesized by stepwise condensation of C2 units to a growing acyl chain. Each elongation cycle results in the addition of two carbons to the acyl chain, and consists of four separate reactions. The first step is the condensation of a C2 unit with the nascent [acp]-bound acyl, forming a a 3-oxoacyl-[acyl-carrier protein] and releasing CO2. This step is followed by reduction to a (3R)-3-hydroxyacyl-[acyl-carrier protein], dehydration to a trans-2-enoyl-[acyl-carrier protein], and a final reduction to form the elongated fatty acid-[acp]. This chain of event repeats itself several times, until the final product, a palmitoyl-[acp], is formed.

There are two basic types of fatty acid biosynthesis systems, named type I and type II. The type I system is found in animals and lower eukaryotes. The mammalian system consists of a single gene product that contains all of the reaction centers required to produce a fatty acid (see fatty acid synthase from Homo sapiens), while the system of lower eukaryotes (such as yeast) consists of two genes, whose polypeptide products combine to form a multifunctional complex (see fatty acid synthase from Saccharomyces cerevisiae). The pathway catalyzed by the type I systems is available at palmitate biosynthesis I (type I fatty acid synthase).

Type II systems are found in bacteria, plants [White05], parasites of the Apicomplexa phylum [Ferguson07] and mitochondria [Zhang03k, Miinalainen03]. The reactions in these systems are catalyzed by a series of individual soluble proteins that are each encoded by a discrete gene, and the pathway intermediates are transferred between the enzymes as thioesters of a soluble [acyl-carrier protein].

Unlike the type I system that accepts malonyl-CoA as the source of the two-carbon units, the type II systems accept only a malonyl-[acp]. The final product of the pathway is palmitoyl group bound to the acyl-carrier protein. It is released from the [acp] moiety and converted to a free acid or a CoA-derivative.

In plant systems palmitoyl-[acp] is often extended to form stearoyl-[acp] (see stearate biosynthesis II (bacteria and plants)). Thus, in most plants the primary products of fatty acid biosynthesis are both palmitoyl-[acp] (16:0-ACP) and stearoyl-[acp] (18:0-ACP). In addition, the enzyme stearoyl-[acp] 9-desaturase, which is ubiquitous in plants, introduces the first double bond in stearoyl-[acp] (while still bound to ACP), producing oleoyl-acp. The palmitate and oleate products can either be utilized within the plastid for lipid assembly and further desaturation, or exported to allow further desaturation in the endoplasmic reticulum, as well as incorporation into lipids. The fatty acids that are exported out of the chloroplast are first cleaved from ACP by thioesterases, followed by activation to fatty acyl-CoAs by an outer envelope LACS9 activity. Once activated to acyl-CoAs, they are exported to the endoplasmic reticulum [Schnurr04].
