Summary from MetaCyc:

A significant amount of the fatty acids synthesized in animals by the cytosolic enzyme complex fatty acid synthase (FAS) or taken up by the diet are further elongated into very long chain fatty acids (VLCFAs), which are at least 22 carbons in length. VLCFAs are also important in plants, where they are the main constituents of hydrophobic polymers (such as cuticular waxes, seed oils and membrane sphingolipids) that prevent dessication at the leaf surface and provide stability to pollen grains [Trenkamp04].

VLCFAs are synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum by sequential addition of C2 moieties from malonyl-CoA to preformed C18 acyl groups derived from the de novo fatty acid biosynthesis pathway. Each elongation cycle is composed of 4 reactions, similar to de novo fatty acid biosynthesis. The cycle includes the condensation of a long-chain fatty acyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA to form a 2,3,4-saturated 3-oxoacyl-CoA, reduction to a 3-hydroxyacyl-CoA, dehydration to a (2E)-2-enoyl-CoA, and a final reduction to yield a chain-elongated fatty acyl-CoA [Jakobsson06].

Unlike in de novo fatty acid biosynthesis, malonyl-CoA rather than a malonyl-[acp] is used in the condensing reaction. The elongation cycle is catalyzed by a membrane-bound enzyme complex, the elongase [Domergue00], which is composed of four enzymes, very-long-chain 3-ketoacyl-CoA synthase or condensing enzyme [Ghanevati01], 3-ketoacyl-CoA reductase, very-long-chain 3-hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydratase and very-long-chain enoyl-CoA reductase [Zheng05b].

Substrate and tissue specificity of VLCFAs elongation is determined by the condensing enzyme, which catalyzes the rate-limiting reaction. Both saturated and unsaturated fatty acids are elongated via this pathway. While both NADPH and NADH were shown to be an effective reducing power for the two reduction steps, in many cases NADPH is the 'preferred' reductant (reviewed in [Cassagne94] and [Jakobsson06]).
