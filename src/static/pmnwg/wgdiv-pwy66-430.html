Pathway Summary

General Background

Fatty acids are key building blocks for the phospholipid components of cell membranes. They are also determinants of intracellular communication in the form of lipid second messengers [Prieschl00] and fatty acyl moieties of proteins that modify their location and function [Resh99].

There are two basic types of fatty acid biosynthesis systems, named type I and type II. Type I systems are found in lower eukaryotes and animals, while type II systems are found in bacteria, plants [White05], parasites of the Apicomplexa phylum [Ferguson07] and mitochondria [Zhang03k, Miinalainen03]. Thus both systems operate in animals - the type I system in the cytosol, (see palmitate biosynthesis I (type I fatty acid synthase)), and the type II system in the mitochondria (this pathway).

The main difference between the two systems is that type I systems consist of one (in the case of animals) or two (in the case of fungi) large multifunctional gene products that form a single complex that contains all of the reaction centers required to produce a fatty acid (see fatty acid synthase from Homo sapiens), while in type II systems the different reactions are catalyzed by a series of individual soluble proteins that are each encoded by a discrete gene, and the pathway intermediates are transferred between the enzymes as thioesters of a soluble [acyl-carrier protein].

About This Pathway

The contribution of the mitochondrial pathway counts for only a few percentage of the overall cellular fatty acid biosynthesis, as the bulk of endogenously derived fatty acids is synthesized in the cytosol by the type I fatty acid synthase system [Brody97]. The mitochondrial pathway is believed to provide the octanoyl group that is required for the endogenous synthesis of (R)-lipoate. Additionally there is putative evidence suggesting its involvement in remodeling of cardiolipins [Zensen92, Schneider95].

The mitochondrial FAS pathway is indespensible, as demonstrated by the observation that the inactivation of any FAS II gene in yeast results in the loss of mitochondrially encoded cytochromes and ultimately in a respiratory-deficient phenotype.

The pathway starts with malonyl-[acp], whose production is described in the pathway fatty acid biosynthesis initiation (mitochondria). The malonyl moiety is attached to the mitochondrial acyl carrier protein, which is encoded by the NDUFAB1 gene. The four steps of the iterative fatty acid biosynthesis process - malonyltransfer, reduction of the oxo group to a 3R hydroxy group, dehydration, which forms a trans double bond, and reduction of this bond, are carried out by the 3-oxoacyl-[acyl-carrier protein] reductase (a heterotetramer encoded by a HSD17B8 and CBR4 genes), a hydroxyacyl-thioester dehydratase type 2 (encoded by HTD2), a mitochondrial trans-2-enoyl-[acyl-carrier protein] reductase (encoded by MECR), and a mitochondrial 3-oxoacyl-[acyl-carrier-protein] synthase (encoded by OXSM).

While all enzymes have been shown to act with substrates having chain lengths up to 16 carbon atoms, the physiological relevance of products longer than octanoate (used as a precursor for (R)-lipoate biosynthesis), however, is not clear. The final product of this pathway is tetradecanoate, based on an observation reported from Neurospora crassa [Mikolajczyk90]. Some studies reported that plant mitochondria also produce palmitate [Wada97] and even stearate [Gueguen00]. Simlar reports have not been made for the mammalian system.
