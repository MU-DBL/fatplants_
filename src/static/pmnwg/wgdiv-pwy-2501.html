Supporting evidence for this pathway in Aegilops tauschii: Enzyme(s) that catalyzes one or more of the key reactions in this pathway were predicted in Aegilops tauschii.

Summary from MetaCyc:

Although β-oxidation is the major route for fatty acid degradation, fatty acids can also be subject to α- and ω-oxidation.

There are several forms of α-oxidation. In this pathway a fatty acid is oxidized at the α-position (C-2) to give rise to a 2-hydroperoxy fatty acid. A different type of α-oxidation, used for the degradation of β-methyl-branched fatty acids, is described at 3-methyl-branched fatty acid α-oxidation.

2-hydroperoxy fatty acids are chemically unstable and are quickly converted to chain-shortened (by one carbon) fatty aldehydes through decarboxylation, or to 2-hydroxy fatty acids. The chain-shortened fatty aldehydes are further oxidized to free fatty acids which can enter the next round of α-oxidation. The conversion of the intermediate 2(R)-hydroperoxy fatty acids to 2(R)-hydroxy fatty acids may be spontaneous or catalyzed by a peroxidase [Hamberg02].

It was reported that pea α-dioxygenase catalyzes both the dioxygenation and the peroxidation activities [Saffert00]. The conversion of the chain-shortened aldehydes to corresponding fatty acids may be spontaneous or catalyzed by an NAD+ oxidoreductase. An enzyme with such activity, described as "a 50 kDa subunit of α-dioxygenase" was described from pea [Saffert00].

Both polysaturated and unsaturated fatty acids are effective substrates in the pathway. Among the tested substrates are laurate, palmitate, stearate, oleate, linoleate, linolenate, and arachidonate.

The physiological role of fatty acid α-oxidation in plants is not clear, but the process has been correlated with responses to both biotic and abiotic stresses, such as wounding and plant-pathogen interactions [De02a, Hamberg03]. It has also been noted that α-dioxygenase in pea is induced only during seed germination [Saffert00].
