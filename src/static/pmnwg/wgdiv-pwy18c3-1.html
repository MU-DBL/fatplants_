Pathway Summary

Until fairly recently the biosynthesis of cholesterol in plants was poorly understood and only a few enzymes involved in this pathway had been characterized [Sawai14]. On the other hand, this pathway has been elucidated in much more detail in animals (see for instance superpathway of cholesterol biosynthesis) [Nes11]. Cholesterol has been found in plants at much lower concentrations than in animals (i.e. 1-2% of the total sterols) but can accumulate to significant higher levels in Solanaceae [Whitaker88, Whitaker91, Japelt11]. The more prominent sterols in plants, which have been investigated in more detail are the phytosterols (C24-alkylsterols) [Piironen00] (see phytosterol biosynthesis (plants)).

The observation that cholesterol level and steroidal glycoalkaloid biosynthesis in potato appeared to be connected [Bergenstrahle96] confirmed previous results [Tschesche80, Heftmann67] and laid the foundation to studies that implicated the involvement of cholesterol in the biosynthesis of steroidal glycoalkaloids [Ohmura95, Ohyama13]. Because of their adverse effects in human diet steroidal glycoalkaloids are considered a health threat [Friedman06], a fact which in view of the advantageous pharmaceutical properties of steroidal glycoalkaloids resulted in considerable efforts to elucidate the underlying pathways of cholesterol, α-tomatine in tomato (see solasodine and soladulcidine biosynthesis, α-tomatine biosynthesis) [Itkin11, Itkin13] and α-chaconine and α-solanine in potato (see α-solanine/α-chaconine biosynthesis) [McCue05, McCue06, McCue07]. Furthermore, cholesterol is an important component of membranes and protective surface layers and gives rise to phytoecdysteroids [Dinan01, Cardenas15].

The finding that cholesterol is usually present in plants in only diminutive amounts but rises to significant higher levels in members of the Solaneceae family suggests that the biosynthesis of cholesterol in those species might be distinct from that of other plants. For instance, in Nicotiana benthamiana the level of cholesterol reaches up to 12% of the total sterols [Suza16]. The cholesterol biosynthetic pathway is the gateway to various steroids and vitamins, e.g. vitamin D3 (see vitamin D3 biosynthesis) [Japelt13]. Metabolic intermediates such as cycloartenol and desmosterol are branching points that either give way to plant C-24 alkylsterols (see phytosterol biosynthesis (plants)) or cholesterol. The flux into different pathways depends on the activity of basically two enzymes competing for those substrates, i.e. the sterol C-24 methyltransferase 1, which steers the flux of carbon towards C-24 alkylsterols and in doing so lowers cholesterol biosynthesis [Diener00, Holmberg02, Arnqvist03], and the sterol side chain reductase (SSR2), which has been shown to form respective precursor and cholesterol by reducing the side chain of cycloartenol and desmosterol [Sawai14].

Using the human 24-dehydrocholesterol reductase (DHCR24) [Waterham01] as probe and performing BLAST searches against the potato unigene database [Quackenbush01] and the tomato database [Bombarely11] resulted in the identification of cDNA's (SSR2) that encoded sterol side chain reductases able to carry out the reduction of cycloartenol and desmosterol to form cycloartanol and cholesterol, respectively [Sawai14].

The SSR2 cDNA of tomato was then used to identify the entire set of cholesterogenesis genes in Solanum lycopersicum and Nicotiana benthamiana and this information lead to the entire reaction sequence required for the biosynthesis of cholesterol in plants [Sonawane16, Cardenas16]. It was found that the enzymes of this pathway are often promiscuous and are active in related pathways such as phytosterol biosynthesis (plants). This is in accordance with the evolutionary 'patchwork' hypothesis, proposing that ancestral enzymes with low substrate specificity develop narrower specificities through duplication and diversification over time, which eventually results in the genesis of new pathways with enzymes of new catalytic properties [Ycas74, Moghe15]. This is exemplified by the C4-demethylation complex formed by sterol methyl oxidases (SMO's), 3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase/C4-decarboxylase (3βHSD), and 3-oxosteroid reductase (SDR) [Pascal94, Nes11, Rahier11]. In tomato and Nicotiana benthamiana SMO3 and 4 encode proteins that are specific for cholesterol biosynthesis, whereas SMO1 and 2 encode proteins that catalyze reactions in phytosterol biosynthesis (plants). Since both groups of SMO's participate in a multi-enzyme complexes with the same promiscuous 3βHSD and SDR proteins, the specificity of the SMO component determines in what pathway, cholesterol biosynthesis (plants, early side-chain reductase) or phytosterol biosynthesis (plants), those enzyme complexes operate [Sonawane16, Cardenas16].
