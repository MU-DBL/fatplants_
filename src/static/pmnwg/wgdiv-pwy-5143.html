Summary from MetaCyc:

Fatty acids are often found in the cell in the activated form of an acyl-coA. Acyl-CoAs are used in the biosynthesis of many cellular products and components. In plants they are involved in the biosynthesis of membrane lipids, seed storage lipids, wax, cutins and suberin.

Intracellular membranes are generally barriers for acyl-CoAs, preventing their movement within the cell. To circumvent this limitation, fatty acids are transported in their free form, and converted to acyl-CoAs while crossing the membrane by the enzymes acyl-CoA synthetases (ACS). These enzymes catalyze the esterification of fatty acids into the CoA thioesters concomitant with transport. In plant cells, ACS activity has been detected in plastids, oil bodies, peroxisomes, mitochondria, and the ER.

The activated fatty-CoAs can be hydrolyzed back to free acids (see acyl-CoA hydrolysis).

The Arabidopsis genome contains nine functional genes encoding long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase - LACS1, LACS2, LACS3, LACS4, LACS5, LACS6, LACS7, LACS8 and LACS9 [Shockey02]. Genetic and biochemical evidence have sorted the physiological roles of some of them.

The plastidial LACS9 contributes to the activation of de novo synthesized fatty acids during their exporting from the chloroplast [Schnurr02]. LACS6 and LACS7 are peroxisome-localized and are involved in fatty acids β-oxidation (fatty acid β-oxidation II (plant peroxisome)) in the peroxisome [Fulda02]. LACS2 is required for cutin biosynthesis, where it was shown to activate the cutin monomer 16-hydroxypalmitate [Schnurr04].
